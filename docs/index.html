Residual interpretation

The residual component does not look like pure white noise. The residual series still shows strong short-run dependence, with lag-1 autocorrelation of 0.86, and the Ljung-Box test rejects a pure-noise process at both 13 and 26 lags (p-values 0.0000 and 0.0000).

The largest leftover disturbances cluster around winter transition weeks rather than appearing evenly spread through the year. Absolute residuals are largest in March, and the most volatile weeks are 10, 8, 9, 11. In the figure, this leftover component is split using an AR(4) residual filter into a Structured Residual line, meant to capture winter weather shocks, storage-regime shifts, and short-memory residual momentum, and a faster-moving Noise line for the remaining short-run variation.

After the split, the Noise component has lag-1 autocorrelation of 0.00. The Ljung-Box p-values for Noise are 0.8266 at 13 lags and 0.9814 at 26 lags. Values above 0.05 support a white-noise interpretation; values below 0.05 indicate remaining serial dependence. The remaining Noise passes this white-noise check at the 5% level, so the STL plus AR(5) residual filter is absorbing most of the visible short-run structure.

Weather conditioning

This section merges NOAA weekly heating degree day data into the EIA storage history using the instructed Friday alignment with a nearest-date tolerance of up to three days. It gives the physical weather context behind large inventory withdrawals and builds.

Local HDD source: noaa_weekly_hdd_full_history.csv. Matched weekly rows: 1692. Raw HDD versus storage-draw correlation: 0.92. HDD anomaly versus storage-draw correlation: 0.29.

The raw HDD relationship stays strong because winter seasonality drives both weather demand and storage withdrawals together. The anomaly relationship is intentionally harder: it removes the normal seasonal pattern and focuses only on unusually cold or warm departures from that baseline. The anomaly now uses a rolling weekly normal: a 30-year average where enough history exists, otherwise a 10-year weekly average.

East HDD is proxied by the mean of New England and Middle Atlantic utility-gas-weighted HDD. Midwest HDD is proxied by the mean of East North Central and West North Central utility-gas-weighted HDD.

Extreme Event Analysis

This panel is the EVT stress-test layer. It uses the full available weekly EIA inventory history from 1993-12-31 to 2026-05-29 (32.4 years) only for tail-risk fitting, while the rest of the dashboard remains based on the 10-year working dataset. The orange bars show the empirical tail, the brown curve is the fitted GPD tail, and the dashed blue curve shows what a normal residual model would expect beyond the same tail threshold.

Selected full-history stress shock: 2014-04-18, -424 Bcf residual (negative), or 4.41 sigma. Tail threshold: 150 Bcf. GPD shape: -0.38 with 90% bootstrap interval -0.50 to -0.24. GPD scale: 121.4.

The week ending April 18, 2014, represents one of the most significant statistical dislocations in the history of the US natural gas market. The reason for this massive residual is that the market was experiencing the hangover of the 2013-2014 Polar Vortex, the most severe winter for natural gas demand in over a decade.

GPD-adjusted probability: the standard normal model implies this shock is roughly 1-in-1860.5 years; the fitted GPD tail implies roughly 1-in-31.4 years, with a 90% bootstrap return-period band of 1-in-15.5 years to 1-in-1167.9 years. The gap is the fat-tail evidence: large storage shocks occur more often than a Gaussian model would suggest.
